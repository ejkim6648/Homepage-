I am a PhD candidate in Sociology at the University of Wisconsin-Madison. My research examines the mutually constitutive relationship between technological development and socio-political relations, with particular focus on algorithmic governance and artificial intelligence.

My dissertation addresses how technological change shapes socio-political relations by examining algorithmic governance in the platform economy. Drawing on computational text analysis and interviews with platform network participants—including YouTube creators, food delivery workers, and freelancers—I analyze how algorithmic systems reshape understandings of rule, rationality, and workplace politics across different platform contexts.

My other articles are published in Korean Labor Studies and Culture and Society in Korean.

Dissertation

In Progress
Algorithmic Governance in the Platform Economy: Reshaping Work and Social Relations

My dissertation examines how technological change shapes socio-political relations through the lens of algorithmic governance in the platform economy. Using computational text analysis and in-depth interviews with YouTube creators, food delivery workers, and freelancers, I analyze how algorithmic systems fundamentally reshape understandings of rule, rationality, and workplace politics across diverse platform contexts. This research contributes to our understanding of the mutually constitutive relationship between technological development and social transformation.
